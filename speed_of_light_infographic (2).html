A History of Scientific Measurement

Chasing Light

How scientists across three centuries measured one of nature's most fundamental constants

c = 299,792,458 m/s  ·  exact, by definition
Key Takeaway · Driving Question Answer

Over roughly 350 years, the methods used to measure the speed of light shifted from crude human timing experiments to astronomical observation, then to precision laboratory optics, and finally to microwave interferometry. Each leap was driven by new technology , including telescopes, spinning mirrors, and lasers, as well as new theoretical understanding, particularly Maxwell's discovery that light is an electromagnetic wave. The story ends with a remarkable twist: by 1983, we had measured c so precisely that we redefined the metre using it, turning a measurement into a definition. This tells us that science progresses not just by getting better answers, but by asking better questions.

c. 1638 Galileo Galilei Observational
Method: Lantern Timing Experiment

Galileo stationed two assistants on hilltops roughly a mile apart, each holding a lantern. One would uncover theirs; the moment the second saw the light, they'd uncover theirs back. The idea was to time the round-trip delay. The result was no measurable delay at all. This was not because light is instantaneous, but because the human reaction time swamped any real signal.

Accuracy: Inconclusive First serious empirical attempt, which proved light was far too fast for simple human timing.
1676 Ole Rømer Astronomical
Method: Eclipses of Jupiter's Moon Io

Rømer noticed that Io's eclipses behind Jupiter arrived about 22 minutes later when Earth was on the far side of its orbit from Jupiter than when it was close. He correctly deduced this delay was due to light having to cross the extra width of Earth's orbit. This was the first demonstration that light travels at a finite speed, which was a genuinely revolutionary idea at the time.

Estimate: ~220,000 km/s (~26% low) First quantitative evidence that light has a finite, measurable speed.
1728 James Bradley Astronomical
Method: Stellar Aberration

Bradley discovered that stars appear to shift position slightly over the year, a phenomenon called stellar aberration. This happens because Earth's orbital velocity "tilts" the apparent direction of incoming starlight, just as rain seems to fall at an angle when you run through it. By measuring this tilt, Bradley calculated the ratio of Earth's orbital speed to the speed of light with unprecedented precision.

Estimate: ~301,000 km/s (~0.4% high) Far more accurate than Rømer; established aberration as a powerful astronomical tool.
1849 Hippolyte Fizeau Terrestrial
Method: Rotating Toothed Wheel

Fizeau shone a beam of light through a gap in a spinning toothed wheel, sending it 8 km to a mirror and back. At just the right rotation speed, the returning beam slipped back through the next gap instead of being blocked by a tooth. Timing this gave the speed of light. This was the first successful entirely terrestrial (no stars needed) measurement.

Estimate: ~313,300 km/s (~4.5% high) Proved light speed could be measured in a laboratory setting, opening the door to refinement.
1862 Léon Foucault Terrestrial
Method: Rotating Mirror

Foucault replaced the toothed wheel with a rapidly spinning mirror. Light bounced off the mirror, travelled to a distant fixed mirror, and came back. In that tiny interval, the spinning mirror had rotated slightly. The tiny angular deflection of the returning beam precisely encoded the speed of light. The apparatus could be used indoors over short distances, making it highly practical.

Estimate: 298,000 km/s (~0.6% low) Greatly refined Fizeau's result; rotating-mirror became the gold standard for decades.
1865 James Clerk Maxwell Theoretical
Method: Electromagnetic Theory

Maxwell unified electricity and magnetism into a single elegant framework. His equations predicted the existence of electromagnetic waves, and crucially, their speed could be derived directly from two measurable electric and magnetic constants. The number that fell out, around 310,000 km/s, was tantalizingly close to measured light speeds. Maxwell concluded that light itself is an electromagnetic wave.

Derived from constants: ~310,000 km/s Unified optics with electromagnetism; light speed became a predicted quantity, not just a measured one.
1907 Rosa & Dorsey Electromagnetic
Method: Electromagnetic Constants

Working at the US National Bureau of Standards, Edward Rosa and Noah Dorsey took Maxwell's theoretical framework and turned it into a precision measurement. By carefully measuring the ratio of electromagnetic units (the ratio of the e.m.u. to e.s.u. of electric charge), they derived the speed of light indirectly from electrical experiments alone, without any optics required.

Result: 299,784 km/s (~0.003% off) Confirmed Maxwell's theory with high precision; electromagnetic methods proved competitive with optical ones.
1879 – 1927 Albert Michelson Optical / Precision
Method: Refined Rotating Mirror

Michelson dedicated much of his career to measuring light speed with ever-greater precision, winning the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1907 partly for this work. He refined Foucault's rotating-mirror method over decades. His final series (1926 to 1927) bounced light between Mt. Wilson and Mt. San Antonio, 35 km apart, achieving a precision that stood for decades. He also designed a 1.6 km evacuated pipe to eliminate air's effect.

Best result: 299,796 km/s (~0.001% off) Set the definitive standard for optical measurement; his precision drove all later refinements.
1958 K.D. Froome Microwave
Method: Microwave Interferometry

Froome at the UK's National Physical Laboratory used microwave interferometry rather than visible light. Microwaves can be handled with much greater phase precision than optical waves, allowing extremely accurate measurement of wavelength and frequency, and therefore speed. His result was so precise that it became the accepted international standard value for over a decade.

Result: 299,792.5 ± 0.1 km/s Shifted measurement from optics into the microwave domain; unprecedented accuracy for the era.
1983 17th CGPM Definition
The End of Measurement: The Beginning of Definition

By the early 1980s, laser-based measurements had made the speed of light more precisely known than the metre itself, meaning the metre had become the limiting factor in accuracy. The 17th General Conference on Weights and Measures made a radical decision: fix the speed of light by definition, and redefine the metre in terms of it.

c ≡ 299,792,458 metres per second (exact)

The metre is now defined as the distance light travels in vacuum in 1⁄299,792,458 of a second. This ended three centuries of measurement, not because precision ran out, but because so much had been achieved that the question itself changed. The speed of light is now a fixed cornerstone of our system of units rather than a quantity to be measured.

Exact by definition, with no uncertainty The metre is now defined by light speed, not the other way round.

Reflection

From Galileo's hilltop lanterns to a laser-defined metre, each generation built on the last, advancing not just in accuracy but also in understanding what light fundamentally is.
